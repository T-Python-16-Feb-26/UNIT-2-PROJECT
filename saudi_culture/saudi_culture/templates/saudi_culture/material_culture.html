Architecture

Saudi architecture tells the story of adaptation and ambition. The traditional Najdi style — with its thick mud-brick walls, geometric parapets, and narrow winding streets — was built to withstand the desert heat. Homes in Jeddah's Al-Balad district feature ornate wooden Rawasheen (bay windows) that catch the coastal breeze. Today, Saudi Arabia's skyline is being redefined by mega-projects like NEOM and the Murabba, blending futuristic vision with deep cultural symbolism.

Najdi Architecture Mud-brick fortresses with triangular crenellations, found in Diriyah and Ushaiger.
Hijazi Rawasheen Carved wooden lattice balconies in old Jeddah, designed for ventilation and privacy.
Modern Vision The Line in NEOM — a 170km mirrored city with zero cars and zero carbon emissions.

Traditional Clothing

Saudi traditional dress reflects identity, climate, and status. The Thobe — a long white garment — is the everyday attire for men, often paired with a Shemagh (red-and-white headscarf) or Ghutra (plain white). For formal occasions, men don the Bisht, a flowing gold-trimmed cloak. Women wear the Abaya as an outer garment, with regional variations in embroidery and color. Beneath these, traditional dresses like the Thob Nashal feature vivid embroidery specific to each region.

The Bisht A cloak of prestige, traditionally worn by leaders and grooms, often trimmed with gold thread.
Regional Abayas Styles vary from Asir's colorful embroidery to the sleek modern designs of Riyadh.
The Shemagh & Igal The red-checked headscarf held by a black cord — an iconic symbol of Arabian identity.

Arts & Crafts

Saudi Arabia's artistic heritage runs deep. Sadu weaving — practiced by Bedouin women — produces vibrant geometric textiles used for tents, rugs, and camel saddles, and is recognized by UNESCO. Arabic calligraphy transforms Quranic verses and poetry into visual art, with styles ranging from angular Kufic to flowing Naskh. Traditional silver jewelry, adorned with turquoise and amber, served as portable wealth for nomadic tribes.

Sadu Weaving UNESCO-recognized Bedouin craft using bold geometric patterns in red, black, orange, and white.
Arabic Calligraphy The art of beautiful writing — from ancient Kufic inscriptions to contemporary street murals.
Bedouin Jewelry Silver necklaces, anklets, and headpieces that encoded tribal identity and family wealth.
