Traditional Instruments

📷The Oud — King of Arabic Instruments
📷The Rebaba — Desert Fiddle
📷The Tabl — War Drum

The Oud (عود) is the soul of Arabian music — a pear-shaped lute with 11 or 13 strings and no frets, allowing the infinite microtones that define the Arabic maqam system. The Rebaba, a single-stringed bowed instrument, carries the haunting melodies of Bedouin poetry. Percussion is central: the Tabl (war drum), Duff (frame drum), and Darbuka (goblet drum) drive the rhythmic pulse of every celebration. The Mizmar (double-reed pipe) provides sharp, penetrating melodic lines in folk ensembles.

The Oud The grandfather of the European lute — Arab traders carried it to Andalusia, changing Western music forever.
The Rebaba A single-string fiddle played by Bedouin poets — its mournful tone is said to mimic the desert wind.
The Duff A shallow frame drum used in celebrations and Sufi rituals — its beat is the heartbeat of Saudi folk music.

The Ardah — Sword Dance

▶

Video Placeholder — Ardah Sword Dance Performance

The Ardah (العرضة) is Saudi Arabia's most iconic performing art — a traditional sword dance performed by two rows of men facing each other, moving in synchronized steps to the beat of drums and the chant of a poet. Originally performed before battle, today it marks every major celebration: Eid, weddings, National Day, and royal ceremonies. The poet (Shair) improvises verses of valor and pride, while men brandish swords or rifles overhead. In 2015, UNESCO inscribed the Ardah on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

UNESCO Heritage (2015) The Ardah is globally recognized as an intangible cultural treasure of Saudi Arabia.
The Shair (Poet) The poet improvises verses during the Ardah — his words set the mood and honor the occasion.
Tabl & Bas Drum Two large drums drive the Ardah's rhythm — their beat can be heard across an entire village.

Regional Music Traditions

📷Hijazi Samri Performance
📷Southern Leiwah Ceremony
📷Eastern Gulf Music

Saudi Arabia's musical landscape is as diverse as its geography. The Samri — a poetic musical tradition from Najd and Hijaz — features call-and-response singing accompanied by clapping and the Rebaba. The southern Asir region has distinctive Mizmar folk ensembles with double-reed pipes played in piercing unison. The Eastern Province features the Leiwah — music with African roots brought by coastal traders. The Hijaz, shaped by centuries of pilgrimage, absorbed musical influences from Turkey, Persia, and Africa.

Samri A poetic tradition from central Arabia — verses are sung, then answered by a chorus, around a fire.
Leiwah East Coast music with deep African roots — rhythms and ceremonies brought by coastal traders.
Mizmar Folk Southern Saudi pipe music — loud, driving, performed at outdoor weddings and harvest celebrations.
